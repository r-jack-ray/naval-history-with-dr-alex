# Summary Quality Contract

Accept a public topic summary only when it satisfies every rule below.

1. Define or identify the subject itself, never the site, archive, videos, results, watch points, or study-guide entries.
2. Reflect the sense demonstrated by every keyed location. The locations establish which dictionary sense is intended; they are not the default authority for the definition when a reliable reference entry exists.
3. Prefer one sentence of roughly 18-32 words; never exceed 220 characters without a recorded approved reason.
4. State the externally verified subject type and the smallest stable useful distinction: affiliation, role, period, or significance only when reliable reference evidence supports it.
5. Omit unsupported nationality, dates, measurements, roles, participants, outcomes, and significance.
6. Reject hollow openings such as `This topic covers`, `Learn about`, `Explore`, `Content about`, and `Related videos`.
7. Preserve general families as general; do not collapse several designs, navies, periods, or roles into one famous member.
8. Block unrelated senses for taxonomy review instead of blurring them.
9. Do not give different retained slugs identical complete summaries.
10. Make the sentence read naturally beneath a Topic heading and on a compact card.
11. For a topic covered by a matching English Wikipedia article, use the article's identification as the primary definitional reference after confirming the corpus uses that sense. Use authoritative specialist sources when Wikipedia is absent, ambiguous, weakly sourced, or insufficiently precise.
12. Treat transcript wording as evidence of usage and context, not reliable ontology. In particular, never classify a topic as a person merely because Dr Clarke personifies it, gives it agency, or uses person-like pronouns or verbs.
13. Write the definition in original language. Do not copy or closely paraphrase a source sentence. If the corpus appears to contradict a stable external fact, first test for a namesake, transcription error, rhetorical phrasing, or taxonomy collision; record unresolved conflicts instead of inventing a compromise definition.
14. Accuracy is a hard gate. An empty pending summary is preferable to a guessed identity, weak namesake match, generic entity template, or polished sentence whose facts have not been established.

## Family prompts

- Generic calibres and weapon categories: define the measurement or family and acknowledge meaningful design or role variation.
- Specific systems: identify type, operator or origin, period, and principal role only when stable across the evidence.
- Named ships and classes: identify vessel type, navy, period, and the distinguishing service or design context demonstrated in the corpus.
- Events: identify the event, participants, place or period, and outcome or significance only when consistently supported.
- People and institutions: identify the role or function relevant to the keyed material, not a full biography or institutional history.
- Concepts: define the operational, strategic, economic, technical, or social relationship that distinguishes the subject.
- Places and political entities: use the historical or strategic corpus sense, not travel-guide prose.
- Books, media, games, and fiction: identify the work or fictional subject accurately and keep it separate from real-world namesakes.

Accepted generic example:

> Naval guns with a nominal three-inch bore; individual designs differed in ammunition, mounting, and anti-ship or anti-aircraft use.

Accepted externally identified example:

> The 1719 Establishment set detailed standard dimensions and scantlings for rated Royal Navy warships, covering vessels from 20-gun ships to 100-gun first rates.

Rejected examples:

- `Watch points covering 3-inch Guns across Dr. Alex Clarke's videos.`
- `Explore study-guide entries on 3-inch Guns.`
- `This topic covers 3-inch Guns in naval history.`
- `1719 Establishment is a person identified by the role, affiliation, actions, and historical context demonstrated in the keyed evidence.`
